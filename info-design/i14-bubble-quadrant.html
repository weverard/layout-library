Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
A scatter of area-scaled bubbles split into four labeled quadrants by two midline rules, with the quadrant that matters most for the headline tinted a faint accent wash.
When to use it
Any time you're positioning a set of options on two axes and want a size dimension to carry a third variable without a second chart — effort/impact, risk/return, growth/share.
Data shape
{label, x, y, size}[], 12 items, x and y normalized to [0,1] against whatever axes you name.
Visual style
Bubble radius is proportional to the square root of size, never to size directly, so area — not diameter — represents magnitude; quadrant corner labels replace an axis legend, and only the focal quadrant's bubbles carry the accent color.

This is a bubble quadrant: two thin hairline rules cross at the midpoint of the plot, splitting it into four quadrants, each named in small uppercase monospace type tucked into its own corner rather than in an axis title or legend. Twelve circles of varying size sit across the four quadrants, their radius scaled to the square root of a third value so that area, not diameter, reads as magnitude. Every bubble not in the quadrant that matters gets a neutral warm-gray fill with a thin white stroke to separate overlaps; the bubbles that land in the one quadrant worth calling out switch to the accent color, and that quadrant's rectangle gets a faint accent wash behind it and its corner label turns accent too, so the eye lands there first. Most bubbles carry their name directly beneath them in small type; hovering any bubble reveals its exact effort, impact, and size values in a small dark tooltip. On narrow screens the direct labels disappear and only the tooltip remains, since twelve labels crowd a narrow chart badly.
