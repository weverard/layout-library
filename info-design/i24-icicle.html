Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
An icicle (partition) chart: a full-width bar on top, its children stacked in a row beneath it with widths proportional to their share, and their children in a third row beneath that.
When to use it
File sizes, budget trees, taxonomy weights — anywhere a hierarchy also carries a size, and both the structure and the proportions need to read at once.
Data shape
{label, depth, x0, x1, parent}[] — a root spanning 0-1 and two levels beneath it; every child row exactly partitions its parent's span, no gaps or overlaps.
Visual style
Depth is encoded as a tint step of the accent — darkest at the top, lighter with each row down. Labels hide in cells too narrow to hold them.

This is an icicle chart: a full-width bar sits on top, a row of narrower bars beneath it whose widths are each proportional to their share of the row above, and a third row beneath that partitioning each of those bars the same way. Every child row exactly tiles its parent's width with no gaps or overlaps, so the eye can read both the tree structure and the size of every branch in one shape. Depth is shown as a tint step of a single accent color — solid at the top row, progressively lighter with each row down — rather than a different hue per level, so the palette stays restrained. Labels sit inside each cell and disappear entirely when a cell is too narrow to hold them rather than overflow or wrap. Clicking any bar below the top row zooms into it: that bar rescales to fill the full width, its own children rescale beneath it by the same proportion, and a breadcrumb trail appears above naming every ancestor back to the root — clicking an earlier crumb, including the root, jumps back out to that level. Under roughly 520px the rows grow taller for easier tapping and labels stop showing by default, appearing only on hover or focus.
