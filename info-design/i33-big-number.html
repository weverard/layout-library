Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
An annotated big-number figure: one hero stat rendered huge, with three margin notes leadered to the specific pieces of context that back it up.
When to use it
Report leads, one-pagers, and posters where a single number carries the whole point and the supporting facts are secondary but still need a citation.
Data shape
One hero stat (value + unit/qualifier), 3 support facts, and a 12-point context sparkline series.
Visual style
An asymmetric, annotation-driven composition — deliberately not a centered, symmetric slide. The number sits huge in serif accent at the left; thin leader lines run from right-margin notes to the sparkline, a comparison chip, and a date, so every claim next to the number is pointed at, not just adjacent to it.

Recreate prompt
This is an annotated big-number figure, not a slide: one hero stat is set huge in a serif accent typeface at the left of an asymmetric grid, with its unit set small beside it. Below the number sits a compact 12-point sparkline with a dot marking its endpoint, and beside it a small comparison chip and a dateline in monospace. In the right margin, three short notes — each with a one-word all-caps label — sit stacked down the page, and a thin line runs from each note to the exact thing it's annotating: one to the sparkline's endpoint, one to the comparison chip, one to the date. The leader lines are computed from the actual rendered positions of the note and its target, so they stay accurate at any width, and the composition reads as an editorial figure — deliberately asymmetric and margin-annotated — rather than a centered, symmetric KPI card. Under about 560px there isn't room for margin notes and leader lines to mean anything, so the notes drop the leaders entirely and stack as a plain, left-ruled list below the number.
