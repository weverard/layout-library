Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
A treemap — tiled rectangles whose area is proportional to value, grouped into three color families for a second level of structure.
When to use it
Budgets, portfolio weights, storage footprints — composition with two meaningful levels (group and item) where both the whole and the parts need to read at a glance.
Data shape
{group, label, value, x, y, w, h}[] — 8 leaves across 3 groups; the rects are squarified by hand ahead of time and hardcoded in a 600x360 unit space, not computed live.
Visual style
One hue family per group — the accent for the lead group, two muted companion hues for the rest — with white gutters standing in for a legend; cells too small for their label fall back to a footnote list under the figure.

Recreate prompt
This is a treemap: eight rectangles tile a canvas edge to edge with a thin white gutter between them, each rectangle's area proportional to its value, and grouped into three column-like regions by category. Each of the three groups gets its own color family — one hue is the chart's accent and marks the lead group, the other two are muted companion hues — and within a family, darker/bolder tiles mark the larger items and lighter tiles the smaller ones, so hue tells you the group and shade tells you the rank inside it. There is no legend: labels are set directly inside whichever cells are large enough to hold a name and a percentage, in white text with a soft dark outline so it reads over any tile color; the handful of cells too small for that instead appear as a short footnote list under the figure. Hovering any tile lifts it — a darker outline and a touch more brightness — and dims the rest slightly, while a chip reports the exact label, value, and share of the total. Below roughly 520px the tiled layout re-renders as a plain one-level horizontal bar list sorted by size, trading the two-level grouping for legibility at a small size.
