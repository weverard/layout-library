Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
A grid of small multiple line charts — one panel per series — deliberately drawn on a single shared y-scale instead of letting each panel auto-fit its own range.
When to use it
Comparing many trends at once without a spaghetti chart or, worse, dishonest per-panel scaling that exaggerates flat lines into cliffs.
Data shape
{label, pts[12], focal?}[] — 12 series x 12 points. The global min/max across all 144 points is computed once and reused for every panel.
Visual style
One flagged focal panel switches its line and border to the accent color; every other panel stays ink on a neutral border; a faint gray dashed line marks the all-series median at the identical y in every panel, so scale-cheating is visually impossible.

This is a grid of twelve small line-chart panels, one per series, deliberately built so every panel shares a single y-scale rather than auto-fitting to its own range — the global minimum and maximum across all twelve series are computed once, and that same mapping draws every line, so a viewer can trust that a taller line really means a bigger number. Each panel carries its label at the top-left and its final value at the top-right in monospace, both inside the chart itself rather than in a caption. A faint gray dashed line marks the median of every point in the whole dataset, drawn at the identical height in every single panel, which makes it trivial to see which lines run above or below the pack. One panel is flagged as the one to watch: its line and border switch to the accent color while the other eleven stay a quiet ink tone, so it reads as different without needing a legend. Hovering any panel enlarges it slightly, opens a small halo around its endpoint, and drops a chip reporting its final value and where it started.
