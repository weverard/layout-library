Spec — what it is, when to use it
What it is
A chord diagram: five entities sit as arcs around a circle, sized by their total flow, joined by ribbons whose width encodes the flow between each pair.
When to use it
Symmetric peer-to-peer relationships — migrations between regions, trade between countries, cross-team collaboration — where every entity can flow to every other one and both the total and the pairwise split matter.
Data shape
arcs[]{label,a0,a1}, ribbons[]{s,t,sa0,sa1,ta0,ta1,v} — 5 entities, a symmetric 5x5 matrix, 10 unique pairs; every angle is precomputed from the matrix and a shared radians-per-unit scale.
Visual style
Arc segments form a thin ring, one muted per entity except the entity with the largest total, which carries the accent. Low-opacity ribbons cross the interior; labels sit just outside the ring, rotated to run tangentially so they stay readable at any position on the circle.

This is a chord diagram: five arc segments form a thin ring around a circle, each arc's length proportional to that entity's total flow, separated by small fixed gaps. Every arc is a muted neutral except the one entity with the single largest total, which carries the accent color, so the eye finds the most connected entity first. Low-opacity ribbons cross the interior of the circle, curving through the center point, connecting each pair of arcs with a band whose width matches the flow between them at both ends. Labels sit just outside the ring, each one rotated to run tangentially along the circle rather than staying horizontal, and flipped upright on the left half of the circle so nothing reads upside down. Hovering an arc fades every ribbon except the ones touching that entity and shows a chip with its total; hovering a single ribbon isolates just that one and shows the exact pairwise value. Under roughly 560px the circular layout gives way entirely to a plain symmetric matrix — a heat-shaded grid table of the same ten values, row and column headers naming every entity, diagonal cells empty — trading the radial form for a layout that stays legible and scannable at a small width.
